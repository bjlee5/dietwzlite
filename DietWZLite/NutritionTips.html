There are many sources of nutrition information, including the DietWZ website. The best source of information is a registered dietitian (with the letters RD or RDN after his or her name) or your doctor, but some helpful, educational tips are included here. The USDA/USDHHS 2015 - 2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans. 8th Edition. December 2015 (referred to as the DGA) is the source of some of the following information. App users should consult their doctor or a registered dietitian before making dietary changes.

Here's a bullet point summary of the key objectives for a person in normal health, but please read further for more details on each:

  • Sodium: DGA recommendation is less than 2,300 mg per day.

  • Sugar: Based on World Health Organization guidelines, limit your daily free sugars intake to 5 to 10% of your total calories (energy) intake. The DGA recommends keeping added sugar below 10% of your daily calories intake. (Sugar is 4 calories per gram.)

  • Saturated fat: Try to limit saturated fat intake to not more than 10% of your daily calories, per the DGA. (Fat is 9 calories per gram.)

  • Fiber: Females: 22 to 28 g daily.
    Males: 28 to 34 g daily.
    (Lower the amount as you age.)

  • Protein: Normally, daily protein intake should be between 0.8 and 1.0 g per kg of body weight.
    DGA recommends:
    46 g protein daily for adult females,
    52 to 56 g for adult males.

  • Carbohydrates: Try to get most of your “carbs”) from healthy sources: whole grains, beans, and fresh or frozen fruits and vegetables.
    Try to keep your daily carbs intake in the range of 45 to 55% of your daily total calories intake.

    • More Info

    IF you have high blood pressure (hypertension), you may need to further reduce your daily sodium intake to less than 1,500 mg. Follow your doctor's orders!

    Limit consumption of added sugar, since excess consumption can lead to weight gain and Type 2 diabetes. For example, if your goal for daily calories intake, is 1,800 calories, a 10% goal for added sugar intake should be not more than 180 calories (about 45 grams). Each gram of sugar is 4 calories, and a teaspoon of sugar is about 4 grams (16 calories).

    Soluble fiber slows the release of food from your stomach to your intestines, so you feel fuller longer. Insoluble fiber is good for digestive health and weight control.

    It takes protein to maintain and build muscle. If you’re very active physically, you’ll need more protein. This is a good reason to consult a registered dietitian or your doctor, as physical activity can increase your daily needs to a range of 1.0 to 1.4 g per kg body weight for recreational athletes, and as high as 1.7 for endurance athletes or regular strength training.

    Also, if you're obese (BMI 30 or higher) and are trying to lose weight, you should consult your doctor or a registered dietitian about possibly increasing your protein intake. Some studies show that increased protein in your diet can help satisfy your appetite.

    More Tips

  • Protein is found in meats, seafood, eggs, beans and peas, soy products, nuts and seeds. These foods contribute B vitamins, zinc, magnesium and iron to your diet.

  • Fill 1/2 of your plate with fruits and veggies, 1/4 with protein, 1/4 with whole grains.

  • Try to consume half (or more) of your grains as whole grain. “Whole grain” means the product contains the whole grain seed – and its nutrients and fiber. Examples: whole-grain breads, crackers and cereals, oatmeal, brown rice.

  • Drink a glass of water before meals. Water helps you feel fuller and it aids digestion.

    And, you need to stay hydrated to stay healthy. Generally, you're fluid intake is probably adequate if you are seldom thirsty and your urine is colorless or light yellow. If you have a medical condition or just aren't sure what your fluid intake should be, consult your doctor or a registered dietitian.

  • Milk and milk products are good sources of calcium, Vitamin D and potassium. (But, they can also have fat and sodium – choose wisely!) Go with fat-free (skim) milk or 1% milk instead of whole milk or 2% for lower fat intake and fewer calories.
